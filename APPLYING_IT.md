# Applying it

You have a direction: palette, type, layout grammar, a voice contract, a banned list. This page turns it into actual work. Principles only, no platform pixel numbers; apps change their crop zones, these don't.

## Before you make anything

Three questions, every single piece:

1. **Which tone word is this piece carrying?** One piece, one word. A reel trying to be all three of your words at once carries none.
2. **What does the voice contract say?** Check the do and the don't for that word before you open the editor, not after.
3. **What's the one idea?** If you can't say it in a sentence, the piece isn't ready to design.

## Reels and video

- The hook carries the feeling before it carries information. A viewer should know your temperature inside the first frame, from colour, type and pace alone. That's the 3-second read, moving.
- Your palette's job on video is the cover frame and the text overlays. Base colour behind text, accent on the one word that matters, never more.
- Pace is part of your identity. If your voice is calm, cuts breathe. If your voice is loud, cuts hit. Cutting fast because everyone cuts fast is wearing someone else's brand.
- Keep text away from the edges generously; every platform eats margins with buttons and captions. If a word matters, it lives near the middle.
- Captions in your voice, not caption-ese. If your transcript wouldn't contain the sentence, the caption shouldn't either.

## Carousels

- Slide one is a promise in your display type, big. It earns the swipe with the feeling first, the topic second.
- One idea per slide, always. The moment a slide makes two points, split it.
- Give the set a rhythm, not just a sequence. Name the worry, walk the logic, land the payoff. However yours goes: the shape should be feelable by slide three.
- Spend your accent once per slide at most, on the word or number doing the work.
- The last slide is yours: wordmark, one line, one ask. If every slide sells, none do.

## Single images and posts

- Your layout grammar decides where things live before content arrives. Decide once, reuse forever; sameness across posts is identity, not laziness.
- The tension move: one choice per piece that feels slightly risky by your own rules. If everything is safe, the piece is furniture.
- Type hierarchy does the guiding. If someone reads only the biggest line, they should still get the idea.

## Anything else

Slides, thumbnails, a menu, a poster, a landing page: same three checks. Which word, what does the contract say, what's the one idea. The direction doesn't care about the format; that's the point of deriving it from voice instead of from a template.

## The exit checks

Run these on the finished piece, before it ships:

- **The 3-second read.** Glance, look away. Did the feeling land?
- **The greyscale test.** Strip the colour in your head. Does the hierarchy survive?
- **The banned list.** Hold the piece against your never-list. Any resemblance, redo it.
- **The removal test.** Take one more element away. Still works? It needed removing. Repeat until removing breaks it.
- **The contract.** Read the do and don't for this piece's tone word one last time.

Five checks, thirty seconds. That's the whole discipline: taste is just reasons, applied every time.
